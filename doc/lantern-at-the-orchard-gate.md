Mira had not meant to stay out so late.

The errand had been a small one, nothing more than returning a blue bowl to old Mrs. Fen, but the evening had the kind of softness that made a person linger. The air was cool without being cold. The last of the light lay over the orchard in long dim bands, and the trees stood in rows like patient listeners.

She took the narrow path home because it was quieter than the road. It ran beside the old stone wall, past the grass-grown posts, and toward the wooden gate no one used anymore.

By day the gate was nothing to look at. It leaned a little to one side. The latch was rusted. Wild ivy had wound itself through the lower bars as if to keep the world neatly divided between one field and another.

But that evening a lantern hung from the top beam.

Mira stopped walking.

The lantern was small and brass-framed, the old-fashioned kind with clear panes and a looped handle. It moved slightly, though there was almost no wind. Inside it, a pale gold flame burned with a steadiness that did not seem entirely natural.

She looked behind her, half expecting to see someone coming up the path. No one did.

The orchard was quiet. A blackbird shifted once in the branches. Somewhere farther off, a dog barked and then changed its mind.

Mira stepped closer to the gate.

She had passed it a hundred times, perhaps more. She knew the silvery grain of the wood, the rough place where the lower hinge had split, the little crescent of iron where the latch ought to have sat straight but never quite did. She was certain the lantern had not been there before.

"That is odd," she said aloud, because the world felt stranger if one did not answer it with words.

The lantern gave no reply, unless a small and almost cheerful brightening could be called one.

She laid two fingers on the gate.

The wood was cool. The lantern was warm.

That, more than anything, made her uneasy. A thing can look unexpected and still be harmless. Warmth suggests intention.

Mira stood very still, listening.

At first she heard only the familiar evening sounds: leaves brushing one another, the dry click of branches settling, the faint distant murmur from a house beyond the rise. Then, beneath those, she thought she heard something else.

Not a voice. Not exactly music.

It was more like the memory of a bell.

A single tone, soft and far off, as though it had been rung in another field many years ago and had only now arrived.

Mira's hand slipped from the gate.

The lantern swayed once, very gently, and she had the peculiar sense that she was being invited rather than warned.

She should have gone home.

There was bread dough waiting under a cloth on the kitchen table. Her mother would wonder where she was. The sky was deepening by the minute.

And yet there are evenings when the world does not seem arranged in the ordinary way. Everything remains where it should be, and still one feels that a seam has loosened somewhere just out of sight.

Mira looked through the gate.

Beyond it ran a strip of path between the last orchard trees and a field of pale grass. She knew that path too. It curved left, then vanished behind a low rise, and eventually joined the road near the mill.

But tonight it did not vanish.

Tonight it continued farther than it ought to have done.

Not by much. Only a little. Only enough for certainty to wobble.

Just beyond the rise, where nothing should have been visible at all, she could see the edge of another light.

It was not gold like the lantern above the gate. It was softer than that, almost silver, as though moonlight had learned how to gather itself into a window.

Mira felt, very suddenly, that if she went home now she would lie awake wondering about it.

That seemed, in its own way, more troublesome than being late.

So she lifted the latch.

It should have stuck.

It never moved without complaint, but this time it opened with a quiet ease, as if someone had repaired it that very morning.

The gate swung inward.

The lantern overhead did not flicker. It only shone with the composed attention of a thing that had finished waiting.

Mira stepped through.

At once the air changed.

It still smelled of grass and leaves and evening earth, but there was another scent beneath it now, one she could not name. Not flowers. Not smoke. Something like rain on old stone, perhaps, or the inside of a room that had been closed for a long time and only just opened.

She followed the path as it curved past the rise.

And there, where no building had ever stood, was a small house with one lit window.

It was not a frightening house.

It was narrow and whitewashed, with a slate roof and a door painted the blue of deep water. A bench sat beneath the window. A clay pot rested beside the step, and in it grew a single rosemary bush, silver at the edges in the failing light.

The whole place had the look of something that had always meant to be found, but only by the person who happened to arrive at the right hour.

Mira stopped at the little gate in its garden fence.

The silver light from the window lay across the path in a pale square.

She did not feel afraid now. Or rather, she felt the kind of fear that lives very close to wonder and may, if handled gently, turn into it.

From inside the house came the faint sound of a chair moving across a floor.

Then footsteps.

Then a hand, small and deliberate, drawing back the curtain.

Mira could not see the face clearly through the glass. Only the outline of someone standing there, as if they had been expecting her.

A moment later, the front door opened.

Warm lamplight spilled out across the rosemary and the stones.

An old woman stood on the threshold, wearing a grey shawl and an expression of complete calm.

"There you are," she said, as though Mira were late for an appointment neither of them had bothered to mention.

Mira held the garden gate with one hand.

"I think," she said carefully, "that I may have come the wrong way home."

The old woman smiled.

"Perhaps," she said. "But not by accident."

Then she stepped aside, making room in the doorway, and beyond her Mira saw a round table, a small lamp, and a blue bowl exactly like the one she had returned an hour before.

The sight of it sent a quiet thrill through her, not because it was frightening, but because it made the evening feel suddenly whole, as if a pattern she had only half-glimpsed had just turned toward her and shown its face.

Behind her, far back at the orchard gate, the brass lantern gave one soft swing in the dark.

Mira drew a breath that felt deeper than the path, the field, and the ordinary road home.

Then she opened the garden gate and went in.
