# The Moonkeeper at Willow Pond

At the end of a narrow lane, beyond the last cottage and the last stone wall, there was a pond where the moon came to rest.

It was not the real moon, of course. The real moon kept to the sky, sailing through the dark in her silver boat. But each night, when the air was still, she sent her reflection down to Willow Pond, and there it floated among the reeds.

Mara was the moonkeeper.

She lived in a small blue house beside the water, with a crooked chimney, a round green door, and three shelves full of teacups that did not match. Every evening, just after supper, she put on her wool shawl, took her lantern from its hook, and walked down to the pond.

“Good evening,” she said to the reeds.

The reeds whispered back in their papery voices.

“Good evening,” she said to the frogs.

The frogs blinked solemnly from their stones.

Then Mara knelt at the edge of the pond and looked into the water. If the moon’s reflection was clear, her work was easy. She would sit on the old bench beneath the willow tree and drink a cup of chamomile tea while the night arranged itself around her.

But some nights, the reflection needed tending.

A fallen leaf might drift across the moon’s pale face. A restless breeze might wrinkle the water. A duck, dreaming with one eye open, might paddle straight through the silver circle and scatter it into trembling pieces.

Mara never scolded the ducks.

“Everyone has somewhere to go,” she would say.

On this particular night, the moon arrived looking rather tired. Her reflection appeared in the pond in soft fragments, caught between ripples and shadows.

“Oh dear,” said Mara gently. “A long journey?”

The moon said nothing, but her light seemed to sigh.

Mara set her lantern on the bank and took out her moonkeeper’s tools: a wooden spoon, a length of blue thread, and a small comb made from polished bone.

First, she used the spoon to lift three leaves from the water. They were brown and curled like tiny sleeping boats.

“Not here tonight,” she told them. “You may rest by the stones.”

Then she drew the blue thread lightly across the surface of the pond, not touching the water exactly, but reminding it of stillness. The ripples slowed. The willow branches stopped shivering.

Last of all, Mara took the little comb and combed the moonlight.

This was delicate work. Moonlight is easily startled. If you rush it, it scatters. If you grab at it, it slips away. But if you move slowly, with no wish except to help, moonlight will sometimes let itself be smoothed.

Stroke by stroke, the reflection gathered.

The broken pieces joined into a round, silver glow.

The pond grew quiet.

The frogs tucked their feet beneath them. The reeds leaned close. Even the old willow seemed to breathe more slowly, her long green hair trailing over the water.

“There now,” Mara whispered. “That’s better.”

The moon’s reflection shone up at her, whole and calm.

Mara smiled and sat on the bench. From her pocket she took a small biscuit wrapped in cloth, and from her flask she poured tea into a cup with a chipped handle. The tea smelled of honey and flowers.

For a while, nothing happened.

And that was the best part of the night.

The stars brightened. The lane disappeared into shadow. Somewhere far off, a gate clicked shut, and then the world became still again.

Mara watched the moon in the pond, and the moon watched Mara.

After a time, a little brown mouse crept from the grass and sat beside her shoe.

“Good evening,” said Mara.

The mouse washed its whiskers.

“Yes,” Mara said. “I thought so too.”

When her tea was finished, Mara rose and brushed the crumbs from her shawl. The moon’s reflection floated peacefully now, round and bright among the reeds.

Before going home, Mara lowered her lantern.

“Rest well,” she said to the pond.

The pond held the moon carefully.

“Rest well,” she said to the willow.

The willow let one leaf drift down, slowly, like a blessing.

Then Mara walked back up the narrow lane to her little blue house. She closed the round green door, hung her lantern on its hook, and climbed into bed beneath a quilt stitched with stars.

Outside, Willow Pond kept watch.

And all night long, the moon slept safely in the water.
