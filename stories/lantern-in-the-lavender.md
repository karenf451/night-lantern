# Lantern in the Lavender

At the edge of the garden, where the lavender leaned over the path, a small brass lantern glowed with a steady amber light.

It had not always been a garden lantern. Long ago, or so it liked to remember, it had hung from the hand of a traveler who crossed hills under moonlight. But now it had a quieter work.

Each evening, when the last teacup was washed and the windows turned dark, the lantern woke.

It listened to the moths brushing past the glass. It watched the lavender bow and rise in the soft night wind. It shone just brightly enough for anyone who might need the path, but not so brightly that it disturbed the dreaming house.

A snail passed by, carrying its silver road behind it.

“Good evening,” said the lantern.

The snail did not answer, but it paused for a moment in the warm glow, which was answer enough.

Above the garden, the stars came out one by one, careful and quiet.

The lantern felt the earth cooling beneath its little iron feet. It felt the night settle around the roof, the trees, the sleeping flowers.

And when even the wind grew drowsy, the lantern lowered its flame until it was no bigger than a golden seed.

“Rest now,” it whispered to the garden.

And all through the dark, the lavender breathed.
